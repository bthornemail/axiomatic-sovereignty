# The Constructed Branches

**Axiom 6 — The Ecosystem Fold**

*Given two points p₁ and p₂ and two lines l₁ and l₂, there is a fold that places p₁ onto l₁ and p₂ onto l₂.*

---

## 1. Forks as Branches

In the computational commons, forks are not failures. They are branches — natural extensions of the commons that respond to specific needs, contexts, or visions.

The ecosystem of forks:
- **Diverse:** Different forks serve different purposes
- **Connected:** All forks share a common origin
- **Competitive:** Forks compete for contributors and adoption
- **Cooperative:** Forks share knowledge and innovations

## 2. The Branch Registry

The branch registry is a catalog of all known forks and their relationships. The registry is:

- **Decentralized:** No single entity controls the registry
- **Verifiable:** Every fork entry can be checked for consistency
- **Optional:** Forks are not required to register, but registration is encouraged
- **Historical:** The registry maintains a history of forks, including deprecated ones

### Registration Protocol

To register a fork:

1. Add an entry to THE_CONSTRUCTED_BRANCHES.md
2. State the fork's name, purpose, and origin
3. Link to the fork's repository
4. State the fork's relationship to the parent commons

### Entry Format

```text
## Fork: [Name]

- **Origin:** [parent fork or commons]
- **Purpose:** [why this fork exists]
- **Language:** [primary implementation language, if any]
- **Status:** [active, dormant, deprecated]
- **Link:** [repository URL]
```

## 3. The Fork Protocol

To fork the commons:

1. Fork the repository (or create a new one)
2. State your reason for forking in this document
3. Link your fork to the parent commons
4. Maintain your fork according to the constitution
5. Register your fork in the branch registry (optional)

## 4. The Ecosystem Topology

The ecosystem is a directed acyclic graph (DAG) of forks:

```text
axiomatic-sovereignty
├── fork-a
│   ├── fork-a1
│   └── fork-a2
├── fork-b
└── fork-c
    └── fork-c1
```

The topology is:
- **Rooted:** All forks trace back to axiomatic-sovereignty
- **Acyclic:** Forks do not create circular dependencies
- **Connected:** Every fork is reachable from the root
- **Distributed:** No single node controls the topology

## 5. The Immune Response

When a threat to the commons is detected, the ecosystem responds through forking:

### Detection

A contributor identifies a threat:
- Centralization of control
- Commodification of the commons
- Stagnation of contribution
- Corruption of the constitution

### Response

The contributor forks the commons:
- Creates a new branch
- States the reason for the fork
- Maintains the constitutional invariants

### Divergence

The fork develops independently:
- Implements new features
- Attracts contributors
- Builds reputation

### Resolution

The threat is neutralized:
- The fork becomes the new standard, or
- The parent commons addresses the threat, or
- The ecosystem finds a new equilibrium

## 6. The Ecosystem Invariant

```text
all forks are sovereign
all forks are connected
all forks are verifiable
the ecosystem is a network, not a hierarchy
the root is always traceable
deprecation is not deletion
```

## 7. The Ecosystem Fold Operation

The ecosystem fold (Axiom 6) places a fork into the topology. It says:

- You are here (position in the DAG)
- You came from there (parent fork)
- You serve this purpose (stated reason)
- You are connected (linked to the commons)

The ecosystem fold is the bridge between the individual fork and the collective ecosystem.
